# mm-how-to-have-a-chance
A data analysis experiment to develop a strategic approach for David to beat Goliath


With March madness starting up, there is a classic, gut-wrenching paradigm we get to observe: the matchup of top dogs vs absolute underdogs. The 16 vs. 1 seed games that on average all end the same way: a lopsided, dominant victory for the #1 seed. Not for nothing, the size of the fight in the dog does occasionally prove to be the x-factor, but what if there was a way to repeatably undermine Goliath’s gameplan? 

The general flow of a college (or any) basketball game concerns itself with trading baskets. Success then is seen as optimizing possessions vs. points on scoreboard. On average, the team that assigned the higher seed also owns the advantage when it comes to player quality, especially at the roster-depth of the starting 5. This simply tilts the court in their favor. If your opponent’s success rate is 3x your own, you won’t win no matter how much fight is in you, dog. Simply put, the talent density of your opponent is not a variable you get to control. Not accounting for this as base truth is a strategic mistake. 

Still, not all hope is lost. We are here to think through a strategy that systematically improves your odds of winning through variables you *do* control.

<aside>

**Hypothesis 1:** even among top seeded teams, there is a greater variance of talent density beyond the starting 5 roster spots.

</aside>

So what are the great equalizers, the variables that both teams can exude control over? There are two: **fouls** and **time** on the clock. 

### Fouls

No matter what, each player on a collegiate team is allotted 5 personal fouls before they foul out of the game. 

If hypothesis 1 is true, then the underdog makes up ground every time a foul is called on their opponent’s top-tier players. The advantage compounds non-linearly every time a top 5 player sits with foul trouble, or is disqualified from the contest entirely.  

<aside>

An underdog’s starting 5 may not stack up against their opponents starting 5, but what about the positions at a depth ≥ 5? 

</aside>

<aside>

The best defense is gluing the opponents best players to the bench. 

</aside>

### The gameplan

While the top seeded team is concerned with optimizing possessions vs. points scored, we will concern ourselves with possessions vs. fouls drawn. Better yet, in analyzing our opponent ahead of time, we will have learned and memorized which of their starting 5 are most prone to foul trouble, and we will structure an offensive plan that progressively exploits this vulnerability in their defense, player by player. 

What’s more, to execute this gameplan, we can start our bench against their starters. As long as every trip down the floor results in a foul, especially on the pre-determined targeted players, it can have been drawn by any player. 

Factors to optimize: 

1. One-and-done defensive stands
2. 1:1 Possessions to Fouls drawn
3. Free throws converted
4. Game time preserved

*Bonus: drawing fouls on defense*

**Breaking down the math**

*First order approximation*

Seeing as the historical status quo of the matchup at hand is a 25-30 point blowout, we have at least 1/2 or 2/3 of the equivalent number of possessions (conservatively, 10-15 possessions) to get them into foul trouble. 

*Second order approximation*

If this game plan is executed, once we’ve reached team foul #5, for all following fouls we will be in the bonus. What this means: 1&1 free throws, and optimizing against the second equalizer, time. When this starts to happen, our “runway” represented by the score differential, starts to shorten much more gradually.

**Breaking down the psychology**

Our goal is to create dissonance. Once 2-3 of the starting 5 players are facing foul trouble, the coaching staff on the other sideline will be faced with a very real wrench. Individual brilliance is not nearly as reliable on a collegiate squad as it is in the NBA, and when they start having to rely on less familiar, predetermined lineups chaos is more likely to ensue. 

Once it does, Goliath will be understandably shaken. Expect overall offensive efficiency and efficacy to drop; turnovers, bad shots, and other bugs that will arise organically. In such a high stakes environment like MM, this can have compounding, negative psychological effects. At this stage, no matter what the scoreboard says, we acquire a new weapon: **shit-talking**.

If we have executed this strategy effectively, regardless of what the scoreboard says, we are winning in the framework we’ve created for ourselves. With any luck, this moment will come with enough time for our rested, more-equally matched up starters to actually do something about it.
